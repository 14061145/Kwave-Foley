Kwave-Foley: Large-Scale Multimodal-Driven High-Quality Stereo Video-to-Audio Synthesis

Jun Wang*, Xijuan Zeng*, Chunyu Qiang*, Ruilong Chen, Nan Li(1), Zihan Li, Feng Deng, Nan Li(2), Liang Dong, Chen Zhang, Di Zhang
Kuaishou Technology
Product GitHub arXiv Benchmark

Abstract

We proposed Kwave-Foley, a large-scale multimodal stereo video sound effect generation model that generates high-quality stereo audio synchronized with video content. Kwave-Foley uses larger-scale multimodal data for training, with a total of 130 million training samples. In Kwave-Foley, we proposed a visual semantic representation module and a video audio-visual synchronization module to align video conditions with audio latent elements at the frame level to improve video semantic alignment and audio-visual synchronization, and together with text conditions, precisely control the generation of sound effects that match the video. In addition, we proposed a cross-modal audio sequence representation method that can achieve high-quality modeling in various scenarios such as sound effects, speech, singing, and music. We also proposed a stereo effect rendering method and gave it a significant sense of space, providing stereo directional rendering capabilities for specific directional targets and moving targets in the video. At the same time, in order to make up for the incomplete types and incomplete annotations of the open source benchmarkmar, we also open-sourced an industrial-level benchmark. Our experimental results show that Kwave-Foley trained with the stream matching objective achieves new audio-visual SOTA performance among public models in terms of audio quality, semantic alignment, and audio-visual synchronization.

Method

The core of Kwave-Foley is a multimodal-controlled flowmatching model. Text, video, and temporally extracted video frames serve as conditional inputs. The multimodal features are then fused via a Multimodal Joint Conditioning module, which feeds into the MMDit Block for processing. This module predicts VAE latents, which a pretrained mel decoder subsequently reconstructs into a monaural mel-spectrogram. The monaural spectrogram is then converted to stereo spectrogram via a Mono2Stereo module. Finally, the stereo spectrogram is passed through a vocoder to generate the output waveform.
